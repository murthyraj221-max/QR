What this policy covers
QR generator, stylish name generator, and site usage
Overview

FlexQR is designed to be lightweight

FlexQR is built as a browser-based tool site. The QR generator lets users create QR codes, and the stylish name generator lets users experiment with decorative text. In normal use, these tools are meant to run on the page without requiring a login or a heavy account system.

Information we may process

Inputs, preferences, and technical data

  • Content you type: for example a URL, text snippet, name, gaming tag, or search term inside the generator pages.
  • Basic technical data: browser type, device type, approximate location derived from IP, pages visited, and similar logs when hosting, analytics, or ads are enabled.
  • Preference data: theme selections, button interactions, or consent choices when those features are implemented.
  • Support messages: if you contact the site through email or a form, the message content and reply details may be stored for support.
How data is used

To run the service and improve quality

Data may be used to show the tool you requested, keep the site working, measure performance, detect abuse, improve usability, and comply with legal or operational needs. If ads are shown, some data may also be used to support ad delivery and frequency management.

Your responsibility

Use the tools carefully and lawfully

Users are responsible for the content they enter, generate, copy, or share. FlexQR is not responsible for how another person uses text or QR content after it is generated. Do not use the tools for fraud, impersonation, spam, harassment, malware, illegal activity, or misleading content.

Advertising, cookies, and third parties
Clear disclosures that match common AdSense expectations
Cookies and ads

Third-party vendors may use cookies

Depending on the services enabled on the site, third-party vendors including ad partners may use cookies or similar technologies to serve ads or measure performance. Google states that privacy policies for publisher sites should disclose that third-party vendors, including Google, may use cookies to serve ads based on prior visits and that third parties may place and read cookies, web beacons, or IP-based signals as part of ad serving.

Consent and regional rules

Consent can matter in some regions

For certain users and regions, Google’s support documents state that publishers must obtain legally valid consent for cookies or other local storage where required, and for collection, sharing, and use of personal data for ad personalization.

Data retention

Keep only what is needed

We aim to keep support messages, logs, and configuration data only as long as needed for site operation, troubleshooting, security, or legal compliance. Some third-party services may retain data according to their own policies.

Security

Reasonable protection, no absolute guarantee

We try to use reasonable technical and organizational measures to protect information, but no website can promise perfect security. Internet transmission and browser storage always carry some risk.

More privacy details visitors can understand

This section is written to be readable, specific, and practical. It is not legal advice.

What happens inside the QR generator

When you create a QR code, the tool is meant to use the content you provide only for that generation step. The purpose is utility, not profiling. If a hosting setup or analytics tool is used, only minimal technical data should be collected to keep the site stable.

What happens inside the stylish name generator

The stylish name generator converts text into presentation styles for display and copying. The entered name or tag is used to show output on the page. A good privacy policy should explain that such input is processed to perform the requested action, not sold or repurposed.

Ads and monetization

If the site uses AdSense or a similar ad system, a privacy policy should disclose that ad vendors may use cookies, and it should be easy for users to find the page. Google’s publisher guidance also says policies may change over time, so publishers should keep up with updates.

User controls

Users should be able to clear browser data, adjust cookie settings, use ad preference tools where available, and stop using the site if they do not agree with the policy. If your site shows consent messages, make the choices clear and easy to understand.
